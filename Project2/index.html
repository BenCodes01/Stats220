Introduction

Our topic of interest is how people learn to network, and we look forward to delving into some of the key insights from our data collection. Learning motivation, theory, practice and reflection are the core ingredients of the learning process. We are interested in how our learning behaviour may be influenced by our personality type, our education and our work preference. In examining our analytics, we notice that the theory and reflection aspect of the learning process is very much influenced by our unique qualities.

As part of our survey design, we followed validation rules for responses such that respondents could only input reasonable values. For example, when asked How many hours would you spend on researching how to network from tutorials, attending workshops or other forms of guidance? the input value is required to be within an acceptable range of 0 to 50. This range accounts for the assumption that some respondents are only practical minded, and prefer a hands-on approach while others may prefer to do extensive research before applying their knowledge.

However, we were unable to obtain tidy data from our categorical response of the preferred type of practical learning as it combined multiple values from the respondents. In example, if we obtain the data that an introvert responds events and clubs, the Google form combines the responses Events and Clubs into a single value Events, Clubs. This clearly indicates data which is not tidy as we have to perform coding operations to separate Events, Clubs into individual values before generating data insights.

Summary Statistics

On average, the respondents would require 8.6 hours of theory preparation before attempting to reach out and gain practical experience.
Out of all the respondents who prefer to spend less time preparing (researching, workshops, etc), the least amount of time required is 0 hour(s).
Out of all the respondents who prefer to study and prepare extensively, the most number of required theory preparation is 30 hours.

Key Findings

The results of the relationship between the amount of preparation hours required and personality type suggests that respondents who identify as introverts are more willing to extensively research and attend workshops on networking before attending social gatherings such as clubs and events or reach out to potential employers on internet platforms. This shows that our personality type affects our learning behavior as introverts tend to prefer to plan extensively.

Reflections are an important aspect of learning and it is an effective process that enables us to make critical changes to our skill. Although the results suggests that the number of respondents who takes time to reflect is almost twice the number of respondents who do not reflect, there were key distinctions between those who identify as introverts and extroverts. Of those who identify as introverts, the number of those who reflect on their learning is comparatively much larger than those who dont. However, the reverse is true for those who identify as extroverts, as the number of those dont reflect on their learning is almost twice as large as those who do. It appears that our personality type contributes significantly to whether we prefer to reflect on our work and analyze the sequences to make changes where appropriate.

Comparatively speaking, of those who currently hold (or pursuing) a bachelors degree, the number of those who reflect on their learning is much larger than the number of those that dont. There appears to be equal proportions of those who reflect on their learning and those who dont for holders of trade certificate or polytechnic diploma.

The factor of personality type may play a significant role in whether we spend less time or countless hours in researching, attending workshops, etc, most would agree that its very important to network. This means that the motivation to learn is not a key issue, as people with different personality types have their own style of learning. Some prefer to rely more heavily on the hands on approach than others, their learning places a greater emphasis on practical experience than learning preparation or reflection. While others may prefer to study and plan, and take time to reflect on their learning.
